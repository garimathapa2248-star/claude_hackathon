Term Decoder

Every financial term you'll encounter — explained in plain English, with context for your specific documents.

Capitalization
When unpaid interest becomes principal
▼
When you don't pay interest while in school, it accumulates silently. At graduation or repayment start, all that unpaid interest gets added to your original loan balance — and you now pay interest on that larger number. On your $8k private loan over 4 years, this adds ~$2,800 you'll pay interest on for 10 years.
Forbearance
Pausing payments — but interest keeps running
▼
A temporary pause on loan payments, usually for financial hardship. Sounds helpful — but interest keeps accruing the entire time. When forbearance ends, all that interest capitalizes onto your principal. Use it only as an absolute last resort and understand the cost before requesting it.
Variable rate
An interest rate tied to market conditions
▼
An interest rate that rises and falls with a market index (usually SOFR). Your Sallie Mae loan at "6.99–14.5% variable" could more than double if rates spike. Always calculate your payment at the ceiling rate, not the floor. This is one of the biggest risks in your current package.
Fixed rate
Locked in forever — no surprises
▼
An interest rate set at origination that never changes regardless of market conditions. Your federal loans are fixed at 5.50%. This is almost always better for student borrowers because you can plan exactly what you'll owe every month for the entire life of the loan.
Grace period
Time before your first payment is due
▼
The window between leaving school and your first required loan payment. Federal loans give you 6 months. Your private Sallie Mae loan has zero grace period — payments begin immediately after you graduate or drop below half-time. Plan your job search accordingly.
Origination fee
A fee taken before you see the money
▼
A fee deducted from your loan before it hits your account. A $10,000 loan with a 1.057% origination fee deposits $9,894 — but you owe $10,000 plus interest. Federal loans charge ~1.057%. Private loans vary. You're borrowing more than you receive.
SAP
Satisfactory Academic Progress — the rule most students don't know
▼
Federal law requires you to maintain a minimum GPA AND complete a minimum percentage of attempted credits to continue receiving aid. At NJIT, failing or withdrawing from too many classes triggers a SAP flag. Your entire financial aid package can be suspended — not just one piece — with minimal warning.
Disbursement
When your aid money is actually released
▼
The date NJIT releases financial aid funds to your student account. Happens at the start of each semester. If your aid exceeds tuition, you receive a refund — but loan portions of that refund must be repaid with interest. Don't spend the refund without knowing where it came from.
Subsidized loan
The government pays your interest while in school
▼
A federal loan where the government covers the interest that accrues while you're enrolled at least half-time and during grace periods. This is significantly better than unsubsidized or private loans. Your $3,500 federal loan is subsidized — no interest builds up until 6 months after you leave school.
EOF Grant
NJ's grant for first-gen students — often missed
▼
The Educational Opportunity Fund is a New Jersey state grant for first-generation, low-income students. It can provide $2,000–$5,000/year on top of TAG. NJIT has a strong EOF program. If you're a first-gen NJ student, email eof@njit.edu to check eligibility — many students leave this money unclaimed.
TAG Grant
New Jersey's main state grant
▼
The Tuition Aid Grant is New Jersey's primary state grant for residents attending NJ schools. It doesn't need to be repaid. You must reapply every year via FAFSA. It's already in your package at $3,500 — but it can disappear if you miss the annual renewal deadline or move out of NJ.
Auto-debit discount
Rate reduction that can vanish permanently
▼
Many lenders offer 0.25% interest rate reduction for enrolling in automatic payments. Sounds small but saves ~$150 over a loan's life. The catch: if a payment fails even once, many lenders permanently remove the discount with no reinstatement option. Use a dedicated account with always sufficient funds.

Visa & Tuition Classification

For international students navigating visa transitions at NJIT — the $16,000/year question.

In-state vs. international tuition — $16,000/year difference at NJIT
If your visa status changes (e.g., H-1B dependent to F-1, or transitioning from dependent to independent status), you may be reclassified as international — unless you proactively submit documentation proving NJ domicile. Many students lose in-state status simply because they didn't know to file.
F-1 students: you may still qualify for in-state rates
F-1 visa status alone doesn't automatically disqualify you from in-state tuition in NJ. If you or your parents have established NJ domicile (not just residence), you may file for in-state classification. This is worth fighting for — it's $64,000 over 4 years.
Document checklist — maintaining in-state status
1
Proof of NJ domicile
NJ driver's license or state ID, lease or mortgage in your name (or parent's name if dependent), and proof the address has been your primary home — not just a mailing address.
2
12-month continuous NJ residency evidence
Utility bills, bank statements, or employer letters showing a consistent NJ address for 12+ consecutive months before the semester you're applying for.
3
Intent to remain in NJ documentation
NJ voter registration card, NJ state tax returns (Form NJ-1040), or a signed domicile declaration form — available from NJIT's Registrar's Office on request.
4
Complete visa status documentation
Copy of current I-20, I-94 arrival/departure record, all USCIS approval notices for current and previous visa statuses, and passport biographic page.
5
Submit to NJIT Registrar — before semester billing deadline
Late submissions are almost never accepted. Email registrar@njit.edu with subject line: "In-State Tuition Classification Appeal — [Your Name] — [NJIT ID]". Include all documents as attachments. Follow up within 5 business days if no confirmation.
International students: also check health insurance waiver deadline
NJIT auto-enrolls international students in university health insurance (~$2,500/yr). If you have comparable coverage, you can waive it — but the window is only 2 weeks at the start of each semester. Missing it locks you in for the full year.

Before you withdraw from a class — read this first
Withdrawing triggers a chain reaction you can't undo
Dropping below full-time enrollment can simultaneously affect your scholarship eligibility, your loan disbursement schedule, your health insurance status, your F-1 visa status (if international), and your SAP standing — all at once, often with no warning. Talk to financial aid before you withdraw, not after.
Refund check ≠ free money
When your aid exceeds tuition, NJIT sends a refund. This feels like extra money — it is not. Most of it is loan money you will repay with interest. Budget it across the entire semester before spending any of it.
